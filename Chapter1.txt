\section{Background and Motivation for the Research}
\label{sec:background}

Object detection --- the joint localisation and classification of all objects present
in an image --- constitutes one of the most fundamental problems in computer vision,
with direct applications spanning autonomous navigation, medical image analysis,
industrial quality control, and security monitoring. The central challenge lies in
simultaneously maximising detection accuracy and minimising computational latency,
particularly under the resource constraints imposed by real-time deployment scenarios.

Significant progress has been made over the past decade. The establishment of
large-scale benchmarks such as MS~COCO~\cite{lin2014coco} and
PASCAL~VOC~\cite{everingham2015pascal}, together with open-source infrastructure
projects including Detectron2~\cite{wu2019detectron2},
MMDetection~\cite{chen2019mmdetection}, and MMYOLO~\cite{mmyolo2023}, standardised
experimental protocols and enabled reproducible comparisons across architectures.
Within this environment, single-stage detectors dominated practical applications:
the SSD family~\cite{liu2016ssd} enabled multi-scale predictions in a single forward
pass, while successive YOLO releases progressively refined the speed-accuracy
trade-off without sacrificing the throughput that defines the YOLO paradigm.
YOLOv4~\cite{bochkovskiy2020yolov4} systematically combined training enhancements
--- Mosaic augmentation, DropBlock regularisation, and DIoU-NMS post-processing ---
under the ``bag of freebies'' methodology, demonstrating that disciplined training
design alone could push accuracy substantially without modifying the inference graph.
YOLOv5~\cite{jocher2020yolov5}, YOLOv7~\cite{wang2023yolov7}, and
YOLOv8~\cite{jocher2023yolov8} refined backbone topology, introduced anchor-free
detection heads, and expanded deployment support, collectively establishing the
modern YOLO ecosystem.

The most consequential recent advances came with YOLOv9~\cite{wang2024yolov9} and
YOLO11~\cite{khanam2024yolo11}. YOLOv9 introduced two architectural innovations ---
the Generalised Efficient Layer Aggregation Network (GELAN) backbone, which aggregates
multiple internal feature paths to preserve gradient diversity across depth, and
Programmable Gradient Information (PGI), which installs auxiliary reversible branches
during training to steer gradients toward information-deficient channels, directly
addressing the feature attenuation that plagued earlier CSP-based designs.
YOLOv10~\cite{wang2024yolov10} demonstrated end-to-end detection without
non-maximum suppression through consistent dual assignment training. YOLO11
introduced C3k2 blocks with dual-convolution bottlenecks and C2PSA position-sensitive
attention at the deepest backbone stage, achieving strong parameter efficiency with
competitive accuracy.

Alongside convolutional detectors, Transformer-based architectures have expanded the
design space significantly. DETR~\cite{carion2020detr} cast detection as a set
prediction problem with bipartite matching, eliminating hand-crafted anchors.
Deformable~DETR~\cite{zhu2021deformable} resolved its slow convergence through sparse
deformable attention. DINO~\cite{zhang2023dino} accelerated training through denoising
queries and improved anchor initialisation. The Swin Transformer~\cite{liu2021swin}
provided hierarchical multi-scale representations through shifted window attention,
becoming a widely adopted detection backbone. Deep residual networks~\cite{he2016resnet}
and Cross-Stage Partial connections (CSPNet)~\cite{wang2020cspnet} --- the direct
architectural ancestors of C2f and C3k2 --- established the foundational design
vocabulary that the entire YOLO family inherits.

A parallel and increasingly important development is the emergence of vision--language
detection frameworks, enabled by the contrastive pretraining paradigm pioneered by
CLIP~\cite{radford2021clip}. GLIP~\cite{li2022glip} unified phrase grounding and object
detection through grounded language-image pretraining, achieving strong open-vocabulary
results. GLIPv2~\cite{zhang2022glipv2} extended this with a unified pretraining
objective combining detection, grounding, and image-text matching.
OWL-ViT~\cite{minderer2022owlvit} demonstrated that pretrained vision-language models
could be directly adapted for open-vocabulary detection with minimal modification.
YOLO-World~\cite{cheng2024yoloworld} brought these capabilities into the real-time
regime by embedding CLIP text features into the YOLO pipeline, achieving practical
inference speeds with open-vocabulary generalisation. Feature-wise Linear Modulation
(FiLM)~\cite{perez2018film} demonstrated that per-channel learned affine
transformations conditioned on external signals --- scaling ($\gamma$) and shifting
($\beta$) --- can selectively recalibrate intermediate representations with $O(N)$
complexity, far more efficiently than self-attention mechanisms.

Despite this remarkable progress, three persistent gaps motivate the present
dissertation. First, cross-generation YOLO benchmarking remains absent under
controlled conditions: each version is evaluated under its own protocol, making
principled backbone selection difficult. Second, backbone feature degradation and
static neck fusion remain jointly unaddressed --- YOLOv9's PGI partially improves
the backbone side, but no real-time detector integrates externally guided semantic
conditioning directly into multi-scale neck fusion. Third, text--visual alignment in
existing real-time frameworks is coarse and scale-agnostic, applying uniform text
signals across pyramid levels that encode fundamentally different spatial and semantic
content.

An important clarification shapes the experimental scope of this work. Chapters~II
and~III address different detection tasks with different evaluation conditions.
Chapter~II benchmarks YOLO architectures on standard closed-vocabulary COCO detection
with 80 fixed categories --- the conventional protocol shared across the YOLO
literature --- where YOLOv9-C achieves $54.1$ \APprimary. Chapter~III evaluates
EnYOLO-World on open-vocabulary detection, where the model must generalise at inference
time to object categories specified by free-form text prompts, never seen during
training. EnYOLO-World-S achieves $43.9$ \APprimary\ under this substantially harder
setting --- a lower absolute number than closed-vocabulary YOLOv9-C, as expected given
both the harder task and the smaller model size (13.4M vs.\ 24.5M parameters). The
appropriate comparison baseline for EnYOLO-World is therefore other open-vocabulary
detectors: YOLO-World-L ($51.0$ \APprimary) and GLIP-T ($51.4$ \APprimary\ at 110.6M
parameters), against which EnYOLO-World-L achieves $52.2$ \APprimary\ with only 26.1M
parameters, demonstrating a superior accuracy--efficiency trade-off.

\subsection{YOLO-Based Object Detection}
\label{subsec:yolo_review}

Before single-stage detectors reshaped the field, object detection was dominated by
two-stage pipelines built around region proposal mechanisms. R-CNN~\cite{girshick2014rcnn}
demonstrated that convolutional features extracted over candidate regions could
substantially outperform hand-crafted descriptors, though the per-region extraction
strategy made it computationally prohibitive. Fast R-CNN~\cite{girshick2015fastrcnn}
and Faster R-CNN~\cite{ren2015fasterrcnn} progressively addressed this by sharing
convolutional computation across the image and introducing the Region Proposal Network
(RPN) to replace the selective search stage, reducing inference time by an order of
magnitude while maintaining strong accuracy on PASCAL~VOC and MS~COCO. Despite these
improvements, the two-stage architecture's sequential pipeline --- proposal generation
followed by classification and refinement --- remained a fundamental throughput barrier
that prevented real-time deployment.

YOLO~\cite{redmon2016yolo}, introduced by Redmon et al.\ in 2016, broke from this
paradigm by recasting detection as a single regression problem. The input image is
divided into a spatial grid, and each cell simultaneously predicts bounding-box
coordinates and class probabilities in one forward pass, achieving near real-time
inference on standard hardware. YOLOv2~\cite{redmon2017yolov2} extended this
foundation with batch normalisation, anchor priors derived from k-means clustering of
ground-truth boxes, and multi-scale training that improved small object recall.
YOLOv3~\cite{redmon2018yolov3} adopted a Feature Pyramid Network
(FPN)~\cite{lin2017fpn}-style structure for predictions at three output scales and
replaced the Darknet backbone with Darknet-53, substantially improving accuracy on
challenging benchmarks.

YOLOv4~\cite{bochkovskiy2020yolov4} redirected attention from architecture to
methodology. By systematically cataloguing and combining training improvements ---
Mosaic augmentation, CutMix, DropBlock regularisation, and DIoU-NMS --- under the
``bag of freebies'' and ``bag of specials'' frameworks, it demonstrated that training
protocol design could yield accuracy improvements comparable to architectural changes,
at zero additional inference cost. YOLOv5~\cite{jocher2020yolov5} translated this
into a clean, modular PyTorch codebase with automated anchor calculation and broad
deployment support across ONNX, TensorRT, and CoreML formats, becoming the most
widely deployed YOLO variant in industry despite the absence of a formal academic
publication.

YOLOv7~\cite{wang2023yolov7} introduced trainable bag-of-freebies training strategies
alongside its Extended Efficient Layer Aggregation Network (E-ELAN) backbone, which
modified gradient flow through computational blocks to increase learning capacity
without altering the inference architecture. YOLOv8~\cite{jocher2023yolov8} modernised
the entire ecosystem with anchor-free decoupled detection heads, a modular C2f-based
backbone, and a unified training API, establishing a new practical standard.
YOLOv6~\cite{li2022yolov6} contributed RepBlock designs that applied structural
re-parameterisation throughout the backbone, demonstrating that multi-branch training
architectures could be algebraically merged at inference for zero-cost capacity gains.

YOLOv9~\cite{wang2024yolov9} represented the most consequential architectural advance
since the introduction of FPN-style multi-scale detection. Its GELAN backbone generalises
the ELAN aggregation strategy by permitting arbitrary computational blocks within the
aggregation path rather than restricting it to plain convolutions, enabling richer
gradient flow and more diverse feature representations at each stage. More significantly,
PGI installs auxiliary reversible branches during training that supply reliable gradient
signals grounded in richer input semantics --- directly addressing the information
bottleneck inherent in deep CSP-based designs. The auxiliary apparatus is discarded at
inference, incurring zero additional latency. YOLOv10~\cite{wang2024yolov10}
subsequently demonstrated NMS-free end-to-end detection through consistent dual
assignments, while YOLO11~\cite{khanam2024yolo11} introduced C3k2 and C2PSA to push
parameter efficiency further. The backbone lineage from ResNet~\cite{he2016resnet}
through CSPNet~\cite{wang2020cspnet} to GELAN provides the architectural vocabulary
through which the Chapter~II benchmark is interpreted and the backbone selection
decisions in Chapters~III and~IV are justified.

\subsection{Multi-Scale Fusion \& VL Detection}
\label{subsec:multiscale_vl}

Multi-scale feature fusion became central to modern detection with the introduction of
the Feature Pyramid Network (FPN)~\cite{lin2017fpn}, which established a top-down
pathway with lateral connections to merge high-level semantics with low-level spatial
detail, enabling strong predictions across a wide range of object scales from a shared
feature hierarchy. The Path Aggregation Network (PAN)~\cite{liu2018pan} augmented this
with an additional bottom-up pathway, creating bidirectional information flow that
allowed fine-grained localisation cues to propagate upward to semantic layers while
high-level context propagated downward. This bidirectional design became a standard
component in YOLOv4, YOLOv7, and YOLOv8. BiFPN, introduced as part of
EfficientDet~\cite{tan2020efficientdet}, extended PAN with learnable scalar fusion
weights to balance pyramid level contributions dynamically, though scalar weighting
operates at a coarse channel-agnostic level and cannot adapt to category-specific or
scene-specific feature characteristics.

The fundamental limitation shared by all these fusion mechanisms is rigidity:
concatenation, element-wise addition, and fixed convolutional weights treat every input
channel identically regardless of scene content or target category. Attention-based
modules partially address this. SE networks~\cite{hu2018se} learn channel-wise
multiplicative recalibration weights through global average pooling and a compact
bottleneck MLP, providing a lightweight mechanism for channel selection.
CBAM~\cite{woo2018cbam} extends this with a spatial attention branch that identifies
discriminative spatial regions. However, both SE and CBAM are self-conditioned --- they
derive modulation weights exclusively from the input visual features, without access to
any external semantic signal that could guide the network toward category-relevant
channels. This circularity limits their effectiveness when the input features have
already been degraded by backbone-level information loss.

Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM)~\cite{perez2018film} offers a qualitatively
different approach. Rather than computing attention weights from the input itself, FiLM
conditions per-channel scaling ($\gamma$) and shifting ($\beta$) parameters on an
external signal --- in this dissertation, frozen CLIP text embeddings. The affine
transformation $V'_{h,w,c} = \gamma_c \cdot V_{h,w,c} + \beta_c$ is strictly more
expressive than purely multiplicative attention (SE) because the bias term $\beta_c$
can shift the feature distribution rather than merely rescaling it, enabling better
alignment with the semantic space defined by the text encoder. Crucially, because
$\gamma$ and $\beta$ are vectors rather than matrices, FiLM maintains $O(N)$
computational complexity where $N$ denotes the number of spatial positions, avoiding
the quadratic cost inherent to self-attention and preserving real-time characteristics.
Despite these properties, FiLM-based conditioning within detection necks was unexplored
prior to the work presented in this dissertation.

Vision--language detection frameworks have established that external semantic guidance
can meaningfully improve detection accuracy and vocabulary generalisation.
ViLD~\cite{gu2022vild} demonstrated that distilling knowledge from pretrained
vision-language models into detection architectures enables recognition of novel
categories not seen during training. GLIP~\cite{li2022glip} unified phrase grounding
and object detection through grounded pretraining, achieving competitive results on
both closed-set and open-vocabulary benchmarks. Detic~\cite{zhou2022detic} extended
detector vocabulary by training on image-level classification labels, providing a
complementary route to open-vocabulary capability. OVR-CNN~\cite{zareian2021ovrcnn}
formally framed open-vocabulary detection as a zero-shot learning problem using
visual-semantic embeddings. OWL-ViT~\cite{minderer2022owlvit} showed that standard
vision-language models can be directly adapted for detection with minimal overhead.
YOLO-World~\cite{cheng2024yoloworld} brought these advances into the real-time YOLO
regime through a Re-parameterizable Vision-Language Path Aggregation Network
(RepVL-PAN), demonstrating that text-conditioned detection is achievable at practical
inference speeds. However, YOLO-World's conditioning applies a uniform text signal
across all pyramid levels, ignoring the fundamental differences between shallow
fine-grained features and deep semantic abstractions. This limitation ---
scale-agnostic conditioning --- is the primary motivation for FiLM-PAN's scale-specific
affine modulation design in Chapter~III.

\subsection{Text--Visual Alignment \& Re-param}
\label{subsec:textvl_reparam}

Modern text--visual alignment for detection builds on contrastive vision-language
pretraining. CLIP~\cite{radford2021clip} demonstrated that jointly training image and
text encoders on web-scale image-text pairs through a contrastive objective produces
representations that transfer broadly across visual tasks, including detection, without
task-specific fine-tuning. Its frozen text encoder --- producing 512-dimensional
embeddings from category descriptions --- has since become a standard conditioning
signal in open-vocabulary detectors, providing fixed semantic priors that guide visual
feature adaptation. The Vision Transformer (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} backbone
underlying CLIP processes the image as a sequence of non-overlapping patches, applying
multi-head self-attention globally to capture long-range dependencies that convolutional
backbones cannot efficiently model.

Prompt learning emerged as an efficient paradigm for adapting large pretrained models to
downstream tasks. In natural language processing, learnable soft prompt tokens appended
to the input sequence of models such as GPT-4~\cite{achiam2023gpt4} and
BERT~\cite{devlin2018bert} enabled task adaptation with minimal parameter
updates~\cite{lester2021prompt}. Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT)~\cite{jia2022vpt}
translated this to vision by prepending learnable tokens to the ViT input sequence,
matching full fine-tuning performance at a small fraction of the parameter cost.
CoOp~\cite{zhou2022coop} and CoCoOp~\cite{zhou2022cocoop} applied learnable prompts
specifically to CLIP's text encoder for downstream recognition tasks, demonstrating
that textual conditioning can be efficiently optimised without modifying the encoder
weights. These methods collectively establish that lightweight learnable adapters
inserted at the input boundary of pretrained models are sufficient to re-purpose
general-purpose representations for specialised tasks --- a principle that motivates
RepRTA's residual refinement approach in Chapter~IV.

Applying text conditioning to a feature pyramid introduces a challenge absent from
classification tasks: different pyramid levels encode qualitatively different
information. The shallow level P3 (stride~8) preserves fine-grained spatial detail
essential for small object detection, while the deep level P5 (stride~32) captures
high-level semantic abstractions suited to large object recognition. A single uniform
text signal applied identically across all scales risks suppressing the fine-grained
spatial cues at P3 or under-conditioning the semantic representations at P5. This
scale-dependent alignment problem, overlooked by YOLO-World's uniform conditioning,
is precisely what Scale-aware RepRTA addresses in Chapter~IV by assigning independent
learnable modulation weights per pyramid level.

Structural re-parameterisation provides a complementary strategy for improving model
capacity without inference overhead. RepVGG~\cite{ding2021repvgg} demonstrated that
multi-branch training topologies --- comprising a $3\times3$ convolution, a $1\times1$
convolution, and an identity shortcut --- can be algebraically merged at inference into
a single equivalent $3\times3$ convolution. This principle captures the spirit of
richer gradient dynamics during training (multi-path gradient flow) while eliminating
the runtime cost of the auxiliary branches. YOLOv7 adopted this through planned
re-parameterised convolutions, and YOLOv6 applied it throughout the backbone via
RepBlock. YOLOv9's RepNCSP blocks extend it further within the GELAN aggregation shell.
The SwiGLU activation function~\cite{shazeer2020glu}, a gated linear unit variant using
a Swish gate, provides efficient non-linearity in the feed-forward components used in
RepRTA, offering stronger feature transformation than ReLU-based alternatives at
comparable computational cost.

The RepRTA module proposed in this dissertation applies re-parameterisation to a new
domain: text embedding refinement for vision-language detection. During training, a
residual SwiGLU feed-forward network refines frozen CLIP text embeddings before they
enter the FiLM-PAN fusion pathway, increasing text representation discriminability with
respect to detection-relevant category distinctions. At inference, this auxiliary branch
is absorbed into a single linear transformation through structural re-parameterisation
--- the refined text embedding is precomputed and cached, incurring zero additional
per-image overhead. This design transfers the ``rich training, lean inference''
philosophy of RepVGG from convolutional feature extraction to semantic embedding
refinement, constituting the central novelty of Chapter~IV.

\section{Existing Problems}
\label{sec:problems}
 
Three unresolved problems emerge from the literature reviewed above, and each of
them maps directly onto a major chapter of this dissertation.
 
The first problem is the absence of a unified cross-version benchmark for the YOLO
family. Although successive YOLO generations are often presented as improvements over
their predecessors, they are typically evaluated under different training schedules,
augmentation pipelines, optimization settings, and hardware platforms. Under such
conditions, performance differences cannot be attributed cleanly to architecture alone.
A reported gain may reflect a stronger backbone, but it may just as easily come from a
longer training budget, a more favorable augmentation strategy, or a different
evaluation environment. This makes it difficult to determine which architectural choices
genuinely improve detection performance and efficiency, and which apparent improvements
are partially artifacts of inconsistent experimental conditions. A controlled
side-by-side benchmark under unified settings is therefore necessary, both to establish
a fair comparison across YOLO generations and to guide principled backbone selection
for subsequent chapters.
 
The second problem is the joint limitation of backbone feature degradation and static
neck fusion in real-time detectors. In CSP-based YOLO backbones, fine-grained spatial
information is progressively weakened as features move deeper through the network,
which can reduce recall for small, low-contrast, or partially occluded objects. At the
same time, the standard PAN neck fuses multi-scale features through fixed operations
that are structurally blind to scene content and insensitive to target semantics. These
two weaknesses interact. If the backbone delivers degraded features, the neck receives
weaker inputs; if the neck remains static, it cannot compensate adaptively for the
loss. YOLOv9's PGI partially addresses the backbone side by improving information
preservation during training, while modules such as SE and
CBAM~\cite{hu2018se,woo2018cbam} provide limited forms of feature recalibration.
However, no existing real-time detector jointly addresses both issues through a unified
design that combines stronger feature preservation with adaptive, semantically guided
fusion. In particular, the integration of external semantic signals, such as text
embeddings from vision-language models, into efficient YOLO-style multi-scale fusion
remains largely unexplored.
 
The third problem is the coarse and scale-agnostic nature of existing text-visual
alignment mechanisms in open-vocabulary detection. In frameworks such as
YOLO-World~\cite{cheng2024yoloworld}, text embeddings are applied uniformly across
all pyramid levels, despite the fact that those levels serve fundamentally different
functions. Shallow feature maps such as $P_3$ retain finer spatial detail and are
especially important for small-object localization, whereas deeper maps such as $P_5$
are more semantically abstract and better suited to large objects and contextual
reasoning. A single uniform conditioning signal cannot optimally serve both extremes
of the feature hierarchy. This limitation is compounded by the fact that frozen CLIP
embeddings are not optimized for detection-specific discrimination. They are strong
global semantic representations, but they are not explicitly shaped for region-level
localization and fine-grained category separation. Moreover, many existing text-visual
interaction modules introduce non-trivial computational overhead, which weakens their
suitability for efficient real-time detection. What is needed, therefore, is a
lightweight mechanism that can refine text representations into a more
detection-aligned form while also adapting their influence across pyramid levels
according to scale-specific feature roles.

\section{Main Contents and Organization of the Dissertation}
\label{sec:organisation}
 
This dissertation addresses the three problems above through three interconnected
studies, unified by the theme of prompt-guided feature conditioning for efficient
object detection.
 
Chapter~I introduces the research background, reviews the relevant literature on
YOLO-based detection, multi-scale fusion, vision--language methods, and
re-parameterization techniques, identifies the open problems, and lays out the
dissertation structure.
 
Chapter~II tackles Problem~(1). Seven YOLO versions --- YOLOv5 through YOLO11 ---
are benchmarked on COCO val2017 under unified experimental conditions. The evaluation
spans detection accuracy (\APprimary, \APfifty, \APseventyfive), scale-specific
performance (\APS, \APM, \APL), and efficiency metrics (parameters, FLOPs, FPS). The
results establish YOLOv9's strengths in feature preservation and YOLO11's potential in
computational efficiency, grounding the backbone choices made in Chapters~III and~IV.
 
Chapter~III tackles Problem~(2) with EnYOLO-World. The framework pairs a
PGI-augmented YOLOv9 GELAN backbone --- for robust feature preservation --- with a
newly designed FiLM-Augmented Path Aggregation Network (FiLM-PAN) that uses frozen
CLIP text embeddings to generate per-channel affine parameters for each pyramid level,
achieving semantically guided adaptive fusion at $O(N)$ complexity. On COCO val2017,
EnYOLO-World-L reaches $52.2$ \APprimary, outperforming YOLO-World-L by $+1.2$ AP
and GLIP-T by $+0.8$ AP with only one quarter of the latter's parameters.
 
Chapter~IV tackles Problem~(3) with three improvements. RepRTA refines CLIP text
embeddings through a SwiGLU-based FFN that adds 1.05M parameters during training and
fuses to zero overhead at inference, yielding $+2.2$ AP. The backbone is swapped to
YOLO11-S for better efficiency ($+0.7$ AP with fewer parameters and FLOPs).
Scale-aware RepRTA then assigns learnable weights per pyramid level for scale-adaptive
text conditioning, bringing the final model to $47.2$ \APprimary\ --- a cumulative
$+3.3$ AP over the EnYOLO-World-S baseline.
 
Chapter~V summarizes the contributions, discusses limitations, and outlines future
directions.
